07In context

The Return of the Centered Layout

After a decade of brutalist edges and full-bleed grids, designers are quietly rediscovering symmetry, generous margins, and the patience of a measured column.

Every few seasons the discipline turns on itself. The asymmetry that felt radical in 2015 now reads as a default, and defaults are exactly what good designers love to break. The newest editorial sites lean into something older: a centered masthead, a single column of well-set text, and the confidence to leave the rest of the page empty.

What makes it work is contrast at the type level. A condensed sans like Barlow Condensed compresses a headline into a tight vertical band, leaving air on either side. Below it, a serif built for screens — Merriweather, with its tall x-height and sturdy slabs — does the slow work of being read for paragraphs at a stretch.

The lesson is not that symmetry is back, exactly. It is that the grid was never the point. The point was rhythm: the cadence of a heading against body, of a figure against its caption, of ink against the paper it sits on. Get the pairing right and the layout almost designs itself.

08Body specimen · Merriweather

400 · RegularReadable at any column width

Merriweather was drawn for the screen first — a transitional serif with a generous x-height, open apertures, and a robust, slightly condensed body that holds its color from captions up to running text. Set at sixteen or seventeen pixels it stays comfortable across long passages, which is why it has become a quiet standard for blogs, journals, and long-form reading apps where the reader is meant to forget the type and simply read.
